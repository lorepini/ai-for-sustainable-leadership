Chapter one · Block 1 · S2 – S3

01Framing AI as a leadership & sustainability challenge

Before we touch structure, people, or projects, the course forces a re-framing: AI is not a tool, it is a planetary supply chain with environmental, social, economic and political consequences — and leaders are not neutral when they deploy it.

Block 1 · concept map

The first two sessions take a deliberately uncomfortable position. AI is not "code"; it is mined minerals, ghost work, water-cooled data centers, regulatory exposure, and reshaped power. The sustainable leader's job is not to maximize one dimension (efficiency, growth) while assuming the rest will sort itself out — it is to manage the tensions between dimensions, across organizational levels, and through time. That is the whole framing of the course, and every later block builds on it.

1. The integrative lens — Hahn et al.

Hahn and colleagues argue that the dominant "business case" view of sustainability hides trade-offs by pretending they are win-wins. The integrative view refuses that move: it insists on three dimensions, three levels, and a dependency through time and space.

Integrative Sustainability Framework Hahn et al. (2014) · S2 Core
Sustainability is the simultaneous management of economic, environmental and social tensions across three levels and across time and space.
  • 3 dimensions — economic / environmental / social must hold together; no trade-off can be "assumed away".
  • 3 levels — individual / organizational / systemic — the same tension shows up in all three at once.
  • Temporal dependency — present actions encode past inequalities and shape future capability.
  • Spatial dependency — orgs are embedded in and co-produce the wider system; "we're neutral" misrepresents the role.
  • Exam move (mock Q2.1) — identify the tension, name all three levels, then name temporal + spatial dependencies before proposing a response.

Worked example · the biased hiring AI (mock Q2.1)

A tech firm's hiring AI cuts 40% of HR cost but down-grades CVs from under-resourced schools. Management says the tool is "neutral" because it reflects historical data.

  • Tension — economic (cost savings) vs social (equity).
  • All three levels — individual (rejected candidates), organizational (the firm's hiring pipeline), systemic (the labor market).
  • Temporal — the AI is past-encoded; it projects historical inequality into the future, so the firm IS responsible going forward.
  • Spatial — the firm co-produces the labor system; "neutrality" is a misrepresentation, not a defense.
  • Why option B is correct — it is the only one that names all three levels AND both dependencies AND refuses the neutrality move.

2. The AI ecosystem — Crawford's planetary supply chain

If sustainability is the lens, Crawford gives us the object it must be applied to. AI is not just inference — it is a four-stage supply chain that runs from extracted minerals through deployment to waste, with circularity only sometimes closing the loop.

AI Ecosystem Map Crawford (2021) · Gambacorta & Shreeti (2025) · S2 Core
AI is not "just code" — it is a planetary supply chain: natural resources → deployment & use → impact & waste → recovery & circularity.
  • Natural resources — minerals, water, energy, land, human labor (data labeling, ghost work).
  • Deployment & use — model training, inference, applications, infrastructure.
  • Impact & waste — carbon emissions, e-waste, water stress, social externalities.
  • Recovery & circularity — reuse, recycling, regulation, redistribution.
  • Exam move — when asked about AI sustainability, name the full ecosystem instead of only emissions.

3. The harms the ecosystem produces

Crawford and Gambacorta & Shreeti taxonomize the harms into four families. The exam move is breadth, not depth: cite two from each family to show you understand AI sustainability is multi-vector.

Sustainability Challenges in AI Crawford / Gambacorta & Shreeti · S2 Core
Four families of harm produced by the AI ecosystem — environmental, economic, labor, and power.
  • Environmental — carbon, water stress, resource depletion, e-waste, environmental injustice.
  • Economic & geopolitical — vendor lock-in, geopolitical risk, hidden liabilities, regulatory risk.
  • Labor & inequality — skill polarization, unequal gains, innovation inequality, reduced diversity, de-skilling, ghost work, job reduction, task erosion.
  • Power & rights — surveillance, consent fatigue, power asymmetries, biased outcomes, social exclusion, loss of trust.
  • Exam move — list 2 from each family to show coverage, not 5 from one.

4. Why even good actors fail — sustainability barriers

Sustainability Barriers in AI S2 Core
Why even well-intentioned actors fail to act sustainably — five structural barriers.
  • Market structure — extreme concentration, high entry barriers, fixed costs, unequal bargaining power.
  • Incentives — misaligned (social/env secondary), short-termism, investor pressure.
  • Regulation — fragmented across jurisdictions, regulatory lag.
  • Supply chain — geographic fragmentation, diffused responsibility.
  • Organization — functional silos, no single owner.
  • Exam move — use "barriers" to explain why sustainable leadership requires system change, not just intent.

5. The role of the sustainable leader

Given the harms and the barriers, what is a leader supposed to do? The course's answer: not maximize a single dimension, but manage the tensions between them. "Exceed narrowly, meet broadly" is the operating principle — pick a focus where you can lead, meet baseline elsewhere.

Role of Sustainable Leaders S2 Core
Sustainable leaders manage tensions across time and stakeholders rather than maximize one dimension.
  • Reduce tensions in the long term — design out conflicts before they recur.
  • Decrease number & magnitude of trade-offs — engineer win-wins where possible.
  • Align interests — across social, environmental, economic concerns at different levels.
  • "Exceed narrowly, meet broadly" — pick a focus where you can lead; meet baseline elsewhere.
  • Exam move — use this in any "what should the leader do?" prompt as the lens.

6. The hard-law spine — EU AI Act risk tiers

S3 shifts from why to how. The EU AI Act is the world's first comprehensive AI regulation; the exam will test whether you can correctly categorize an AI system by its risk tier. Obligations scale with risk — and the classic trap is assuming human-in-the-loop downgrades the tier (it does not).

EU AI Act — Risk Tiers EU (2024) · S3 Core
The EU classifies AI systems by risk level; obligations scale with risk.
  • Unacceptable risk — banned outright (social scoring by governments, real-time biometric ID in public, manipulative AI, emotion recognition in workplace/education).
  • High risk — strict obligations: conformity assessment, risk management, human oversight, logging, transparency (employment/recruitment, education, credit scoring, law enforcement, critical infrastructure, biometrics).
  • Limited risk — transparency obligations only (chatbots disclose AI, deepfake labeling).
  • Minimal risk — no obligations beyond voluntary codes (spam filters, AI in video games).
  • Exam move (mock Q1.1) — recruitment/HR AI = HIGH risk. The trap: human-in-the-loop does NOT downgrade the tier.

Worked example · Q1.1 anatomy

"A large employer introduces an AI system to screen job applicants ... recruiters typically follow the AI recommendations, although they can override them."

  • Domain — employment / recruitment → listed as HIGH RISK in Annex III of the Act.
  • Distractor A ("minimal because humans decide") — trap: human-in-the-loop is a HIGH-risk obligation, not a downgrade.
  • Distractor D ("unacceptable") — wrong because it's CV scoring, not emotion recognition in the workplace.
  • Distractor E ("falls outside the Act") — wrong: in-scope as a deployer of high-risk AI.
  • Answer — C. High risk because it is used in employment-related decision-making that may significantly affect individuals.

7. The normative spine — OECD principles

OECD AI Principles OECD (2019) · S3 Key
Five non-binding principles for trustworthy AI, adopted internationally.
  • Inclusive growth, sustainable development & well-being — AI for people and planet.
  • Human-centred values & fairness — respect rule of law, human rights, diversity, equity.
  • Transparency & explainability — disclose AI use, make outputs understandable.
  • Robustness, security & safety — sound throughout lifecycle, traceable, manageable risk.
  • Accountability — organizations responsible for functioning + adherence to the above.
  • Exam move — cite OECD when the question asks about international/normative frameworks.

8. The philosophical synthesis — Floridi & Cowls

Across 47 published AI ethics statements, Floridi & Cowls find one consistent shape: four classic bioethics principles plus a fifth, AI-specific one — explicability, the enabler that makes the other four possible.

Floridi & Cowls Unified Ethics Framework Floridi & Cowls (2019) · S3 Key
Five principles that synthesize 47 published AI ethics statements.
  • Beneficence — promote well-being, preserve dignity, sustain planet.
  • Non-maleficence — privacy, security, capability caution.
  • Autonomy — human power to decide, to delegate, and to revoke delegation.
  • Justice — promote prosperity, preserve solidarity, avoid unfair discrimination.
  • Explicability — enables the other four (transparency + accountability).
  • Exam move — the "5th principle is the enabler" framing — explicability ≠ a goal, it's the precondition.

9. The full toolkit — four families of AI ethics responses

Ethical AI Landscape — 4 Framework Families S3 Key
The full toolkit of AI ethics responses; questions often ask which family applies.
  • Regulatory & legal — EU AI Act (hard law).
  • Normative & principles-based — OECD, UNESCO, company AI principles (soft norms).
  • Process & lifecycle — responsible AI lifecycle, ethics-by-design, model cards, data sheets.
  • Governance & accountability — AI ethics committees, AIA / DPIA, HITL, HOTL, red-teaming, audits.
  • Exam move — when asked "what would you do?" list responses from at least 2 families.

10. The leadership theories the course inherits

S3 closes by importing four leadership traditions and asking which ones survive the AI context. Each is a "voice" you can cite when scenarios demand vision, service, integrity or courage.

Transformational Leadership Bass & Riggio (2006) · S3 Key
Leaders inspire followers through vision and values rather than transactional reward.
  • 4 I's — Idealized influence, Inspirational motivation, Intellectual stimulation, Individualized consideration.
  • Why it matters for AI — sets a vision that integrates ethics into the why, not just the what.
  • Exam move — use as the "vision-setting" anchor in change scenarios.
Servant Leadership Dierendonck & Nuijten (2011) · S3 Key
Leader prioritizes follower development and stakeholder welfare over self-interest.
  • 8 dimensions — empowerment, stewardship, humility, accountability, courage, forgiveness, authenticity, standing back.
  • Why it matters for AI — counters power-asymmetry risk of AI by centering the people affected.
  • Exam move — pair with sustainability questions about employee voice and stakeholder care.
Ethical Leadership Brown et al. (2005) · S3 Key
Demonstration of normatively appropriate conduct through personal actions and interpersonal relationships, plus promotion of such conduct to followers.
  • Moral person — traits + behavior the leader embodies.
  • Moral manager — communicates ethics, rewards/disciplines, sets the climate.
  • Why it matters for AI — "tone at the top" decides whether ethics policies become real.
  • Exam move — cite when the question is about climate or culture of responsible AI.
Moral Courage Edmondson (2020) · Hannah & Avolio (2010) · S3 Key
The capacity to act on ethical convictions despite risk of loss.
  • Required for "speaking up" — in psychologically unsafe AI deployment contexts.
  • Connects to psychological safety — courage is needed where safety is absent.
  • Exam move — use to explain why ethics policies fail without leadership courage.
Building AI Ethics Capability Wade & Tomoko (2024) · Blackman (2022) · S3 Supporting
Ethics has to be an organizational capability, not a checklist.
  • Translate principles into practical guidance — turn "fairness" into testable criteria.
  • Integrate into AI design and development — embed in lifecycle, not append at end.
  • Adapt to local conditions — context-specific implementation.
  • Share best practices across organization — institutional learning loop.
  • Multidisciplinary AI ethics committee — tech + business + legal + affected groups.
  • Exam move — cite when answering "how do we move from principles to practice?"
New Leadership for AI Czakert & Wietrak (2025) · S3 Supporting
Existing leadership theories treat ethics as a supplemental virtue; AI context needs ethics as foundational.
  • Old framing — ethics added on top of governance.
  • New framing — ethics designed into the system; leaders accountable at systemic level.
  • Exam move — cite as the "we need a new model" voice when contrasting traditional leadership theories.
"AI is not just an artifact — it is a planetary system of extraction, labor, infrastructure and governance. Studying its sustainability means studying the supply chain that produces it." Crawford · Atlas of AI · 2021

Exam moves · Block 1

  • If the prompt says "carbon footprint is the only issue" → refute with Hahn et al.: three dimensions, three levels, plus temporal & spatial dependency.
  • If the prompt names AI sustainability → unfold the four-stage ecosystem (Crawford) and the four harm families.
  • For any "what should the leader do" → quote "exceed narrowly, meet broadly" as the operating principle.
  • For any EU AI Act tier question → first identify the domain (HR, education, credit, law enforcement = HIGH), then check for the human-in-the-loop trap.
  • For "how do we make ethics real?" → list responses from at least two of the four ethics families (regulatory + governance is a safe pair).
  • For climate/culture questions → cite Brown et al. ethical leadership; for speak-up & whistleblowing → add Edmondson moral courage.

Q1. A consultant argues that the carbon footprint is the only AI sustainability issue. Which framework refutes this, and how?

Hahn et al. (2014) — the integrative sustainability framework demands all three dimensions (economic, social, environmental) across three levels (individual, organizational, systemic) and through time and space. Carbon is one slice of one dimension. To complete the picture, layer Crawford's AI ecosystem: harms run across four families — environmental, economic, labor & inequality, and power & rights.

Q2. A bank deploys an AI credit-scoring system and adds a "human reviewer" to approve all denials. The compliance team says this makes the system "limited risk" under the EU AI Act. Are they right?

No. Credit scoring is classified as HIGH risk under Annex III of the EU AI Act. The presence of a human-in-the-loop is in fact a high-risk obligation, not a downgrade. Limited-risk obligations cover transparency only (e.g., chatbots must disclose they are AI). The compliance team has fallen into the canonical mock-Q1.1 trap.

Q3. Why does Floridi & Cowls call explicability the "enabler" of the other four ethics principles?

Beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy and justice all require that the AI's behavior can be understood and audited. Without explicability, you cannot tell whether the system is doing good, avoiding harm, respecting consent or producing fair outcomes. Explicability is therefore not a goal alongside the other four — it is the precondition that makes them operational. Cite this framing when a scenario asks whether transparency is a value or a requirement.

Chapter two · Block 2 · S4 – S7

02Designing AI organizations

Now that we know why AI is a sustainability problem, the next question is structural: where does AI capability live inside the company, who builds vs. buys it, and how do we choose which projects to back?

Block 2 · concept map

Block 2 walks four questions in order: How is the org shaped? (S4) — What kind of AI lab fits our strategy? (S5) — Make, buy or partner? (S6) — How do we manage a portfolio of AI projects? (S7). The thread is the same tension you'll see recurring all course: speed vs. consistency, exploration vs. exploitation, local vs. global.

1. Org structure basics — the frame everything else hangs on

Organizational Structure — Basic Forms S4 Core
The frame of reporting relationships that divides + coordinates work.
  • Functional — grouped by expertise (R&D, marketing); efficient, deep specialization, weak cross-coord.
  • Divisional — grouped by product/region/customer; high autonomy, duplication of resources.
  • Matrix — two reporting lines (e.g., function + project); flexible, prone to conflict and slow decisions.
  • Differentiation & Integration — structure must both divide work and coordinate it.
  • Exam move — name the form, then say what it OPTIMIZES (control / speed / specialization).

2. Where does AI live? Vertical vs. Horizontal teams

Vertical (Embedded) vs Horizontal (Centralized) AI Teams S4 Core
Two patterns for placing AI capability inside a company; each has a signature trade-off.
  • Vertical / Embedded — AI lives inside business units; high contextual relevance, weak consistency.
  • Horizontal / Centralized — AI sits in a central function; standards + scale, but distance from business.
  • Three tensions — speed vs consistency · local relevance vs global standards · business ownership vs technical ownership.
  • Exam move — diagnose the org's pain (slow rollouts? inconsistency? irrelevant pilots?) → prescribe the form.

3. Alternatives to hierarchy

Alternative Structures — Amorphous & Holacracy S4 Key
Modern responses to the rigidity of functional/divisional/matrix.
  • Amorphous — fluid, unstructured; teams form/dissolve around problems.
  • Holacracy — structured self-management; rules + roles replace managers.
  • Pros — autonomy, faster decisions, adaptability, transparency, reduced bureaucracy.
  • Cons — complex to implement, steep learning curve, role confusion, doesn't suit regulated industries.
  • Exam move — useful when question asks for "alternative to hierarchy".
Organizing Paradox & Job Deconstruction S4 Key
Balance the need for structure with the need for flexibility by matching skills to tasks dynamically.
  • Job deconstruction — replace fixed roles with skill-task matching.
  • Skills-first organizations — Delta Airlines example; recruit on skills not credentials.
  • Open Opportunities (U.S. federal gov) — internal marketplace for skills.
  • Exam move — cite when question is about future of work or AI augmentation.

4. AI lab archetypes — explore vs. exploit, made structural

An AI lab is the company's bet on where AI capability concentrates. Three archetypes — centralized, decentralized, venture — and no single one is best. The right choice depends on whether the strategic intent is to exploit existing strengths or to explore new ones.

AI Lab Archetypes S5 Core
Three structural patterns for AI labs; each encodes trade-offs between speed, coherence, accountability and time horizon.
  • Centralized Lab — pools scarce talent, standardizes practice, reports to senior leadership. Strong control, risks distance from business.
  • Decentralized / Embedded Lab — AI inside business units. High contextual relevance, weak coordination + consistency.
  • Venture / Incubator Lab — start-up logic: speed, autonomy, external orientation. Built for exploration, not incremental improvement.
  • No archetype is superior — each is a trade-off.
  • Exam move — diagnose the org's strategic intent first (explore vs exploit) → choose archetype.

5. Hybrid labs & social alignment

Hybrid AI Labs & Social Alignment S5 Core
Hybrid labs explicitly pursue both business and social objectives — alignment is structural, not cosmetic.
  • Why they emerge — external pressure: regulation, scrutiny, ethics, legitimacy.
  • What alignment means — embed social/env considerations into HOW problems are selected, HOW success is measured, HOW trade-offs are resolved — from day one.
  • Exam move — alignment ≠ a CSR layer; if it's added after the fact, it isn't alignment.

Case · Musk vs OpenAI · hybrid labs gone wrong

OpenAI was founded as a non-profit "for humanity"; structural drift toward commercial monetization (and Microsoft's billion-dollar bet) produced the very alignment failure the founders feared. The lawsuit alleges the social mission was bolted on, not built in. Cite as the canonical case of broken hybrid alignment.

6. Innovation theatre — when AI is mostly PR

Innovation Theatre S5 Core
Organizations adopt the symbols of innovation without changing the underlying power, incentives, or decision rights.
  • The symbols — impressive lab spaces, demos, mission statements, polished prototypes, bold AI strategies.
  • What doesn't change — decision rights stay concentrated, incentives still reward short-term exploitation, pilots never reach production.
  • The risk — erodes credibility, misallocates talent, delays genuine capability building.
  • Not deliberate deception — emerges from unresolved tensions (explore/exploit, signaling/operational, long-term/short-term).
  • Exam move — name when an "AI initiative" is mostly PR; pair with hybrid alignment to show real vs theatre.

7. Make / Buy / Partner — the sourcing decision (mock Q3.1)

This is the heart of Block 2 and one of the five mock-exam-tested topics. The exam will hand you a context (size, time, talent, core competency, budget, regulatory posture) and ask you to choose. The answer is rarely about which is "best" — it is about which fits the diagnosis.

Make / Buy / Partner — Sourcing AI Capabilities S6 Core
Three paths to acquire AI capability, each with a signature risk profile.
  • MAKE (build) — control, contextual learning, protects assets, differentiation possible, adaptive governance. BUT: slow, expensive, talent constraints, risky (obsolescence).
  • BUY (license vendor) — quick, lower upfront, risk shifted, focus on core. BUT: capability erosion, opacity, dependency, switching costs, misaligned vendor incentives, blurred accountability, regulatory exposure, ethical delegation without control.
  • PARTNER (co-develop) — shared experimentation, complementary capabilities, cross-domain learning, flexibility. BUT: complex management, power asymmetries, ambiguous governance, IP/data lock-in, different priorities.
  • Exam move (mock Q3.1) — diagnose: core/non-core? time? talent? values? Then pick — non-core + speed + no infra = BUY; strategic differentiator = MAKE; complementary expertise needed = PARTNER.

Worked example · RetailNow chatbot (mock Q3.1)

RetailNow has 200 employees, no AI team, no ML infrastructure, three-month timeline, core competency in logistics not technology, limited budget, board is allergic to "open-ended technology bets."

Diagnostic: Non-core capability (logistics is the moat, not chatbots). Speed-critical (holiday season). No talent, no infra. Budget-constrained. Aversion to long bets.

Conclusion: BUY a third-party solution.

  • Why not MAKE — no team, no infra, no time, no budget; building would mean betting the company on a non-core capability.
  • Why not PARTNER — co-development requires complementary contribution, but RetailNow has no AI/ML to contribute; the asymmetry would create a partner-dominated relationship without the speed advantage of BUY.
  • Why BUY fits — quick deployment, low upfront cost, risk shifted to vendor, lets RetailNow keep focus on logistics. Acceptable trade-offs: dependency on vendor, opacity, blurred accountability for outputs — manage with SLAs, exit clauses, escalation pathways for misclassified inquiries.
  • Sustainability note — BUY does not absolve RetailNow of harm caused by the chatbot (Hahn spatial dependency). They still own the customer-facing outcomes, even though they don't own the model.
AI Innovation Strategies — Four Paths Bouquet et al. (2026) · S6/S7 Key
Strategic typology of AI innovation across organizations.
  • Four paths mapping the build/buy/partner spectrum to strategic intent.
  • Use — assess where an organization sits and where it should move.
  • Exam move — pair with Make/Buy/Partner to give a strategic framing on top of the transactional one.

8. Portfolio management — from one project to many

Portfolio Management Kloppenborg & Lanning (2012) · S7 Core
Centralized management of multiple projects to meet strategic business objectives.
  • Portfolio = collection of projects that can be measured, ranked, prioritized.
  • Portfolio mgmt = identifying, prioritizing, authorizing, managing, controlling projects to achieve strategy.
  • Executive tasks (5) — Identify · Select · Prioritize · Resource · Govern.
  • Hierarchy — Strategy development → Portfolio mgmt → Project mgmt.
  • Exam move — distinguish PROJECT mgmt (delivering one) from PORTFOLIO mgmt (allocating across many).
Portfolio Stress-Testing — Scenario Lenses S7 (NordSteel case) Key
Four pressure questions to expose hidden fragility in an AI portfolio.
  • Cost shock — if API/compute costs rise 25%, does the portfolio still work?
  • Regulatory shock — if new explainability rules hit, are you exposed?
  • Competitive shock — if a competitor launches a proprietary foundation model, what happens?
  • Identity shock — what type of company does your portfolio imply you'll become in 5 years?
  • Exam move — use as a checklist to evaluate any portfolio scenario in the exam.
"Project management delivers one project. Portfolio management decides which projects deserve to live at all." Kloppenborg & Lanning · 2012 · S7

Exam moves · Block 2

  • For any org-structure prompt — name the form, then say what it OPTIMIZES (efficiency / autonomy / flexibility).
  • For Vertical vs Horizontal AI — diagnose the org's pain first (slow rollouts → vertical; inconsistency → horizontal).
  • For lab archetype choice — explore vs exploit decides between venture and centralized; speed-to-impact decides between centralized and embedded.
  • If the prompt talks impressive demos but no production use — name it INNOVATION THEATRE.
  • For Make/Buy/Partner — always diagnose by four pressures: core? time? talent? values? Then pick.
  • For portfolios — do not just list five tasks. Apply at least one stress-test (cost / reg / competitive / identity).

Q1. A bank's centralized AI lab ships a fraud-detection model that the retail BU refuses to adopt because it "doesn't fit how we actually work." Diagnose the structural issue and prescribe.

This is the canonical horizontal/centralized failure mode: standards and scale produced, but the lab is distant from the business. The pain is irrelevance, not inconsistency. Two prescriptions: (a) move to a vertical/embedded structure where AI lives inside the BU and gets contextual relevance; (b) keep centralized standards but add embedded translators (AI translator role, S8) inside each BU to bridge. Cite Vertical vs Horizontal teams (S4) as the diagnostic frame.

Q2. NordSteel is choosing between an in-house AI for predictive maintenance (core competency) and a generic vendor solution. Which sourcing path, and why?

MAKE. Predictive maintenance is operationally close to NordSteel's core business; the contextual data (equipment, historical failures) is the moat. Buying would surrender differentiation and create dependency on a vendor whose roadmap is set elsewhere. The downsides of MAKE (slow, expensive, talent constraints) are tolerable because the upside is sustained competitive advantage. Cross-reference Make/Buy/Partner (S6) + Bouquet et al. four paths (S6/S7): this is the "differentiate" cell, not "efficiency."

Q3. A company has launched five flashy AI pilots in 18 months. None has reached production. What is the diagnosis, and what would Stouten et al. say?

This is innovation theatre (S5): the symbols (pilots, demos) are present but the underlying decision rights, incentives and processes did not change — so pilots cannot graduate. Stouten et al. (2018) would say: the change effort skipped the foundational steps — assess the opportunity, mobilize energy (AMO), empower others, identify short-term wins that become routinized. Without those, pilots become PR not capability. The fix is structural: move decision rights, change incentives, build the production pipeline before launching pilot 6.

Chapter three · Block 3 · S8 – S12

03People & leadership in AI organizations

Structure tells you where AI lives; people tell you whether it actually works. Block 3 asks: what roles exist, how do you recruit for them, what motivates the humans who run AI systems, and what happens to the human mind when AI sits next to it?

Block 3 · concept map

The block opens with crisp definitions you need before discussing roles at all (S8), moves through recruitment vs. selection (S9), then deep into motivation theories (S10), people analytics (S10–S11), and finally to Kahneman and the cognitive risk of working alongside intelligent systems (S12). The thread: AI does not replace humans, but it reshapes their work — and reshaping work breaks implicit promises if leaders aren't deliberate.

1. The five concepts most companies confuse

Task / Role / Job / Authority / Profile S8 Core
Five distinct concepts confused at most companies; clarity here drives accountability.
  • Task — what is done (concrete action).
  • Role — responsibility in the system (what the person owns).
  • Job — formal position in the organization (the HR designation).
  • Authority — decision rights (what the person can decide alone).
  • Profile — who can perform the role successfully (skills + personality).
  • Exam move — when a job description is unclear, name which of these is missing.

2. The role map for AI organizations

AI Team Role Map S8 Key
Four families of roles in AI organizations.
  • Core Technical — Data Scientist, ML Engineer, MLOps Engineer, Data Engineer, Technical Lead / AI Architect.
  • Product & Integration — AI Product Owner / AI PM, AI Translator, Domain Expert (SME), UX / Human-Centered AI Designer.
  • Governance & Risk — AI Governance Lead, Compliance/Legal, Responsible AI/Ethics Officer, Model Risk Manager, Data Protection Officer.
  • Strategic & Leadership — Chief AI Officer / Head of AI, CTO, Business Sponsor, Chief Data & AI Officer (CDAIO).
  • Exam move — diagnose missing roles when team gaps show up.

3. Recruitment vs. selection — attract, then choose

Recruitment vs Selection S9 Key
Two complementary HR processes that must align with org strategy.
  • Recruitment — attracting and identifying candidates; goal: create awareness + attract diverse range.
  • Selection — evaluating and choosing candidates; goal: hire the best fit (job + culture).
  • Key principle — alignment of both with organizational goals AND employee expectations.
  • Exam move — they sound similar; the distinction is attract vs choose.
Quality of Hire & RippleHire S9 Supporting
AI-driven recruitment platform showing what ethical AI in HR looks like.
  • Quality of hire = measurable post-hire performance + retention + cultural fit.
  • Ethical AI in recruitment — explainability, bias auditing, candidate experience.
  • Exam move — cite RippleHire as a positive case (vs Q1.1 hiring AI as a high-risk one).

4. Motivation — four theories the exam loves

S10 packs four motivation theories. They overlap; the exam tests whether you can pick the right lens for the right scenario.

Self-Determination Theory (SDT) Deci & Ryan · S10 Core
Intrinsic motivation requires three psychological needs to be met.
  • Autonomy — feeling of volition over actions.
  • Competence — feeling effective in challenges.
  • Relatedness — feeling connected to others.
  • Exam move — diagnose why AI tools demotivate workers (e.g., undermining autonomy/competence).
Flow Theory Csikszentmihalyi · S10 Key
Optimal experience occurs when challenge matches skill.
  • Flow zone — high challenge × high skill.
  • Boredom — low challenge × high skill (canonical risk when AI takes the interesting parts of a job).
  • Anxiety — high challenge × low skill (canonical risk when AI rollouts skip training).
  • Exam move — use for AI job redesign questions (where should AI take routine tasks?).
Job Characteristics Model Hackman & Oldham · S10 Core
Five core characteristics drive motivation and satisfaction.
  • Skill variety — range of skills required.
  • Task identity — visible whole task.
  • Task significance — impact on others.
  • Autonomy — freedom in how.
  • Feedback — direct info on performance.
  • → produce experienced meaningfulness, responsibility, knowledge of results → motivation, performance, satisfaction.
  • Exam move — use when asked how to redesign a job AI is reshaping.
Job Demands–Resources Model Bakker & Demerouti (2006/2007) · S10 Core
Stress and burnout emerge from a perceived imbalance between demands and resources.
  • Demands — responsibilities, pressures, obligations, uncertainties at work.
  • Resources — things within the individual's control to resolve demands.
  • Imbalance — chronic demand > resource → stress → burnout.
  • Connected research — Karasek & Johnson (1986), Le Blanc et al (2008).
  • Exam move — diagnose AI-workplace burnout (e.g., AI overload) with this model.

5. The promises that AI quietly breaks

Psychological Contract Rousseau (1995) · S10 Core
Individual's belief about the terms of a reciprocal exchange with the employer.
  • Promise — explicit or implicit, both parties.
  • Consideration offered — what each gives in return.
  • Reciprocal obligations — bind the parties.
  • Why it matters with AI — AI changes job content; the implicit promise may break ("I was hired to think, now I supervise the model").
  • Exam move — use when an AI rollout causes morale collapse without obvious explanation.
Psychological Safety Edmondson (1999) · S10 Core
Shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking.
  • Required for — speaking up about errors, asking questions, suggesting changes, taking risks.
  • In AI contexts — critical for flagging AI errors, model bias, deployment risks.
  • Exam move — link to moral courage (Edmondson 2020); without safety, courage is solitary.

6. People Analytics — the discipline

7. Kahneman — the cognitive architecture AI tests

S12 takes the lid off the human side of human-AI interaction. Kahneman's System 1 / System 2 split is the lens; cognitive surrender is the failure mode.

Kahneman — System 1 / System 2 Kahneman (2002 Nobel) · S12 Core
Two modes of thinking that explain systematic biases in human judgment.
  • System 1 (Fast) — automatic, emotional, intuitive, effortless (face recognition, loud noise reaction).
  • System 2 (Slow) — deliberative, logical, effortful (hard math, narrow parking).
  • Implication — humans are NOT the rational decision-makers traditional economics assumed.
  • AI implication — AI substitutes for System 1 speed; the danger is humans skip System 2 oversight.
  • Exam move — cite when asked about AI augmentation, bias, or decision quality.
Cognitive Surrender ("Confidently Wrong") S12 Core
When AI is accurate, humans improve a lot; when AI is wrong, humans drop hard — they trust the model even when it misleads.
  • Mechanism — over-reliance on AI; human oversight collapses into rubber-stamping.
  • Risk — automated decision becomes worse than either human or AI alone.
  • Mitigation — keep humans in deliberative System 2 role; design for constructive friction.
  • Exam move — use to argue against passive HITL designs; human oversight must be active.
AI for Leadership — Benefits & Trade-offs S12 Supporting
How AI augments leadership work, and where it risks displacing judgment.
  • Benefits — better decision quality + speed, less routine, more time for strategy + people, AI coaching, tailored communication, frequent feedback, bias audits, workload monitoring.
  • Trade-offs — cognitive surrender, loss of judgment muscle, surveillance of teams, opaque criteria, accountability dilution.
  • Exam move — pair benefits with trade-offs; never one-sided.

Case · Unity Health · cognitive surrender in clinical AI

Clinicians using an AI triage tool started accepting the AI's recommendations even when their own examination pointed elsewhere. The fix wasn't more AI — it was redesigning the workflow so the clinician had to record their independent judgment before seeing the AI score. Constructive friction restored System 2 oversight.

Q1. After a GenAI rollout, sales reps at an insurance firm complain they "just check boxes now." Engagement scores drop 22%. Which motivation framework best diagnoses this, and what's the fix?

Multiple lenses apply, but the cleanest single diagnosis is Job Characteristics Model (Hackman & Oldham): skill variety, task identity and autonomy have all dropped because the AI took the discretionary parts. Workers fall into the BOREDOM quadrant of Flow (high skill, low challenge).

Add Psychological Contract (Rousseau): they were hired to advise; their job now is to supervise the model — the implicit promise has been broken.

Fix: redesign jobs so reps own the complex / edge cases the AI defers to them (rebuilds skill variety + autonomy + significance), and explicitly re-contract: name the new role aloud, do not pretend the job is unchanged.

Q2. A People Analytics team produces a beautiful dashboard showing turnover by department. Leadership thanks them. Is this "people analytics"?

Strictly, no — it is reporting, not analytics. Reporting tells you what happened (turnover was 15%). Analytics (Ferrar & Green) tells you why (new sales hires lack effective onboarding) and what next (here's the onboarding redesign that would have prevented it). The dashboard is a useful artifact but it sits at level 1 of the activities scale; level 4 is advanced analytics & AI driving recommendations. Use this distinction to argue that "people analytics" maturity is measured by recommendations acted on, not dashboards built.

Q3. A trading firm deploys an AI signal that traders are supposed to override when they disagree. Six months in, override rate is 2%. Should they celebrate or worry?

Worry. This is the canonical pattern of cognitive surrender (Kahneman / S12). The AI is now doing both System 1 (speed) and de facto System 2 (judgment), because traders have stopped deliberating. When the AI is right this looks like success; when it is wrong it produces the "confidently wrong" failure mode — worse than either human or AI alone.

Design fix: add constructive friction — require the trader to record an independent confidence rating before seeing the AI score; flag cases where override rate per trader drops below a threshold; rotate traders through "AI-off" days to keep judgment muscle alive.

2. Change models — the classic, the playbook, the individual lens, the evidence-based

Lewin's 3-Step Change Model Lewin · S14 Core
The simplest, oldest change framework: unfreeze, change, refreeze.
  • Unfreeze — disrupt the status quo, create readiness.
  • Transition — implement the change.
  • Refreeze — embed the new state as the norm.
  • Exam move — name when asked the "classic" change model; pair with critiques (too linear).
Kotter's 8-Step Change Model Kotter · S14 Core
The action playbook for large-scale change.
  • 1. Establish a sense of urgency (weak scientific support — Stouten et al. critique: urgency causes fear & rejection).
  • 2. Form a powerful guiding coalition.
  • 3. Create a vision.
  • 4. Communicate the vision.
  • 5. Empower others to act on the vision.
  • 6. Plan for and create short-term wins.
  • 7. Consolidate improvements and produce more change.
  • 8. Institutionalize new approaches.
  • Exam move — name the 8 steps; flag that urgency creation is contested.
ADKAR Model Hiatt · S14 Core
Individual-level change model — change happens through individuals.
  • A — Awareness of the need to change.
  • D — Desire to participate and support.
  • K — Knowledge of how to change.
  • A — Ability to implement required skills/behaviors.
  • R — Reinforcement to sustain.
  • Exam move — cite as the individual lens vs Kotter's organizational lens.
Stouten et al. — Evidence-Based 10 Steps Stouten et al. (2018) · S14 Core
A synthesis of change research that critiques Kotter and proposes evidence-based steps.
  1. Assess the opportunity/problem motivating change (replaces "sense of urgency" — urgency causes fear/rejection).
  2. Select and support a guiding change coalition.
  3. Formulate a clear compelling vision.
  4. Communicate the vision (43% less resistance if explained — Shaw et al. 2003).
  5. Mobilize energy for change (Ability · Motivation · Opportunity — Ajzen 1991).
  6. Empower others to act (procedural justice, voice — Oreg, Tyler & Blader).
  7. Develop and promote change-related knowledge and ability (self-efficacy → motivation — Kao 2017).
  8. Identify short-term wins as reinforcements.
  9. Monitor and strengthen the change process (feedback, course correction).
  10. Institutionalize change (routines embedded — Edmondson; Rerup & Feldman).
  • Exam move — anchor change essays here; it's the evidence-based update to Kotter.

Worked example · Q1.3 anatomy

"A multinational ... middle managers are resisting ... spreading skepticism ... Senior leadership has limited time and wants a solution that quickly reduces resistance, even if it may create some dissatisfaction in the short term."

4 Foundations to Overcome Resistance S14 Core
Beyond tactics, four organizational conditions that reduce resistance.
  • Develop positive relationships — supervisor trust, change-agent trust, supportive environment → more positive feelings about change.
  • Cultivate psychological safety — safe interpersonal risk-taking → info sharing, learning, satisfaction.
  • Implement changes fairly — procedural justice; even if outcome is negative, impact is softened.
  • Select people who accept change — high openness, positive self-concept, risk tolerance, growth need, intrinsic motivation, internal locus of control.
  • Importance of "local tribes" — social systems matter for the change process.

4. Project management methodologies — predictive vs adaptive

Project Management Methodologies Map S15 Core
Two families: plan-driven (predictive) vs adaptive (agile); pick by uncertainty + stability of requirements.

7. Decision rights, the decoupling problem & governance archetypes

Decision Rights in AI — 3 Layers S17 Core
AI decisions sit at three levels; each has a different owner.
  • Strategic — Should we deploy AI at all? → Board / C-suite.
  • Structural — How is AI integrated and constrained? → Business unit leads, product teams.
  • Operational — What does AI decide in real time? → Delegated (often invisibly) to the algorithm.
  • Exam move — pair with the decoupling problem to show where accountability evaporates.
The Decoupling Problem S17 Core
Classic principle: decision rights + accountability travel together. AI breaks this.
  • Traditional — person who decides owns the outcome → accountability is clear.
  • AI-enabled — decision rights delegated to AI; accountability stays with humans → but WHICH human? for WHICH decision?
  • Result — accountability becomes diffuse.
  • Exam move — use as the diagnostic frame before naming a governance fix.
Three Governance Archetypes S17 Core
Three structural patterns for AI governance, each with a clear strength + weakness.
  • Centralized — central AI council/CAIO approves all deployments. STRENGTH: consistent standards, clear accountability. WEAKNESS: bottleneck — slow.
  • Federated — business units own AI decisions within central policy. STRENGTH: speed, domain expertise, scalable. WEAKNESS: policy drift, inconsistency, accountability gaps.
  • Embedded — governance baked into the AI system (guardrails, automated checks). STRENGTH: scales to volume, consistent, fast. WEAKNESS: who audits the guardrails?
  • Exam move — pick by org's pain (speed → federated; rigor → centralized; volume → embedded).

8. RACI (mock Q1.2) — the accountability map

This is the third mock-exam-tested topic. The student is expected to know exactly what each letter means and, critically, why people confuse R and A.

RACI Framework S17 Core
Maps accountability for any decision; four roles, only ONE is accountable.
  • R — Responsible — does the work; CAN be multiple roles; responsibility CAN be delegated.
  • A — Accountable — owns the outcome; MUST be exactly ONE role; CANNOT be delegated; answerable when things go wrong.
  • C — Consulted — provides input BEFORE the decision; two-way communication; influences but doesn't veto.
  • I — Informed — notified AFTER the decision; one-way communication; not involved in making it.
  • Exam move (mock Q1.2) — Responsible = the DOER. The trap: many confuse with Accountable (the OWNER, signs off).

Worked example · Q1.2 anatomy

"Which statement best describes what it means to be 'Responsible' in a RACI framework?"

  • Distractor A ("signs off and ultimately answers for success/failure") — that is Accountable, not Responsible. The classic confusion.
  • Distractor C ("informed after the task is completed") — that is Informed.
  • Distractor D ("must be consulted before decisions") — that is Consulted.
  • Distractor E ("defines project scope and assigns roles") — that is a project sponsor / RACI author, not a RACI role.
  • Answer — B. The person who performs the work or carries out the task.

9. Why RACI breaks at AI scale — the Air Canada lesson

Why RACI Breaks at AI Scale Air Canada chatbot · S17 Core
Three places RACI fails when AI is in the loop.
  1. R when AI acts — AI generates outputs in real time, no human involved. RACI assumes R is a human who can be trained/replaced. R defaults to the team that built the system, but they made a design decision weeks ago, not a judgment about this specific output.
  2. A when AI causes harm — A must be a single role; AI cannot legally hold A. Air Canada tried to put A on the chatbot ("separate legal entity"); the tribunal said no, pushed it back to the org. Most organizations haven't answered which internal role holds A.
  3. Scale breaks C and I — at 500 AI decisions/day, you can't Consult anyone on each. Customer-facing teams + legal cut out → problems accumulate undetected.
  • Exam move — Air Canada is the canonical case; if asked about RACI + AI, this is the story.

Case · Air Canada vs the chatbot · the canonical RACI failure

A grieving passenger consulted Air Canada's customer-service chatbot about bereavement fares. The chatbot fabricated a policy that contradicted the airline's actual terms. The passenger booked, then Air Canada refused the refund. In court, Air Canada argued the chatbot was a "separate legal entity" — the tribunal rejected the argument and held the airline accountable.

The RACI lesson: (1) Responsibility for the output had no human owner (the AI acted). (2) Accountability was attempted to be delegated to the AI — legally impossible. (3) At scale, the Consulted (legal, policy) and Informed (customer service) roles were cut out of every individual output. Result: harm accumulated until a court forced the org to own it.

"Decision rights and accountability are supposed to travel together. AI breaks the link — and the organizations that haven't redesigned RACI find out the hard way." S17 · synthesis · Air Canada

Q1. A CEO insists "we need urgency" before launching an AI transformation. The change consultant disagrees. Which scholar would the consultant cite, and what would they say?

Stouten et al. (2018). They explicitly replace Kotter's first step ("establish a sense of urgency") with "assess the opportunity/problem motivating change." Q2. An AI ethics committee approves a customer-service chatbot. Three months later it hallucinates a refund policy and a customer sues. Who held "A" in the RACI? What does Air Canada teach?

Air Canada tried to put A on the chatbot as a "separate legal entity"; the tribunal said no and pushed A back to the organization.

How to answer a scenario MCQ (3 points each, ~6 min)

  1. Read the full stem twice. Scenario MCQs hide the wrong-answer triggers in the stem (the trap on "neutral tool", "human reviewer", "limited time", "complementary expertise"). Don't read options first.
  2. Name the framework being tested. Mock Q2.1 = Hahn et al. Mock Q1.1 = EU AI Act. Mock Q1.2 = RACI. Mock Q1.3 = Kotter & Schlesinger. Mock Q3.1 = Make/Buy/Partner. The exam reuses these.
  3. Predict the correct answer before reading options. If your prediction matches one of the five, that's almost certainly it. If it matches none, re-read the stem — you missed a constraint.
  4. Eliminate options that miss a dimension. In Hahn questions, eliminate options that name only one level or skip temporal/spatial. In Make/Buy/Partner, eliminate options that ignore the core/non-core diagnosis.
  5. Watch the "all of the above" / "both A and B" pattern. Often used when A and B individually fail the same criterion (mock Q1.3 distractor D).
  6. If two options remain — pick the one that is more specific to the framework. Generic language usually = wrong.

How to structure a 5-point essay in 10–15 minutes

Five points means the grader is looking for five hooks. Give them clearly.

  1. Lead framework (1 line) — "This is a [Make/Buy/Partner | RACI | Hahn | resistance] question." Name it explicitly.
  2. Apply to the scenario (2 lines) — pull the 3 most relevant constraints from the stem and map them to the framework.
  3. Bulleted body (4–6 bullets) — one bullet per option / dimension / step. Bold the conclusion in each.
  4. Cite the author (one mention is enough) — Hahn et al. (2014), Kotter & Schlesinger, Crawford (2021), Ferrar & Green (2021), Stouten et al. (2018), Edmondson (1999), etc.
  5. One real-course case (optional, 1 line) — Air Canada, ManCo, NordSteel, RetailNow, Helix Operations, Unity Health, Musk vs OpenAI, RippleHire.
  6. Conclusion sentence — explicit recommendation in plain language; flag one risk to manage. Do NOT just summarize.

Anti-patterns to avoid

  • Listing without diagnosing. "Here are five things..." with no recommendation = 2/5 max.
  • Citing without explaining. Naming Hahn without unpacking dimensions/levels = 1/5.
  • One-sided answers on AI & leadership. Always pair benefits with trade-offs (cognitive surrender, surveillance, accountability dilution).
  • Forgetting the spatial/temporal dependency. The Hahn distractors all fail this — "the bias existed before us" is a trap.
  • Confusing R and A in RACI. R does, A owns. One A per decision. Cannot delegate A.
  • Defaulting to "human-in-the-loop fixes everything" — under EU AI Act, HITL is an obligation, not a downgrade. Under cognitive surrender (Kahneman), passive HITL fails.
  • Recommending MAKE for non-core capabilities. RetailNow doesn't build a chatbot foundation model; it buys.
  • Recommending coercion when commitment matters. K&S coercion is fast but produces resentment; only use when constraints explicitly tolerate it.
  • Picking "centralized governance" for everything. Centralized creates bottlenecks; federated drifts; embedded scales but needs auditors. The pain decides.
  • Treating sustainability as an environmental issue only. Hahn + Crawford = three dimensions, four harm families, three levels. Coverage > depth on one.
